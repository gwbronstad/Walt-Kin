Otis's Fatther, Gulbrand was a Norwegian immigrant. Gulbrand followed his brother, Christian Olson Bronstad, to the United States after Christian had become established in these United States.


Otis's Mother, Laura was first generation American, her Mother, Petra (Foss) Hansen Was born in Vestre Norway and Immigrated with her Father, Johannes Foss, and Mother, Berta Foss, to Four Mile, TX. Laura's Father, Hans Jorgen Hansen, was an immigrant from Denmark.

Otis's Father, Gulbrand Olson Bronstad, and Selma's Father, William Telief Tergerson, cooperated in many civic endevors such as the founding of the Bank in Cranfills Gap, and in relocating Cranfill's Gap. Their spirit found permanent expression in the marriage of Otis (Gulbrand's oldest child) and Selma, (William T's youngest child).

I W. T. Tergerson was boren in Brownsboro[,] Henderson County[,] Texas in the year 1859 Feb 1st, My parenes Tellef Tergerson & Asber Tergersen Nee Telfeson both parens in Amlje (parish) Norway, Moved in the year 1850 to America came over in A Sale Shipp to New Orlens. Thene to Screvsport thene To Brownsboro in an Ox Wagon, My Father had two Brothers Yearn, Aslak Tergerson that had setled in Brownsboro about 4 years befour.

My Father had bin married twice one Daughter Betsy with first Wife mared to Ole Anderson, Second Wife Asber, they had 9 Childrens two died in Enfince one Girl died and was bured at See, one Boy two yers older Bured in Brownsboro Cemmitery.

I am the Baby boy of the Fameley I was raised on Corn Bred and Bacon three times A day but groed up to be a strong man. My schooling was verry limited I mooved to Bosque Co. when I was 14 years old and my time was occupied in ridin wild Horses and driving Oxens so thir was no time for school. But I had some Sunday School and what My blesed Mother had lerend to me at her Knees whan I was smal and I thank the lord for this. It has fided me on the way to eturnel Life.

My Father died when I was onley 12 years old and Mother was left with three Dawter and two Boyes Famley, Dauters Salley, Sareh, Telisbty, Boys Terry, I. The other three Dauters were all maried Gurice Grimland Died in Brownsboro during the Civil ware. Betsy Anderson Gunil Knudsen both had moved to Bosque about 6 years befour we mooved. Thes two Daughters insisted on Mother to moove to Bosque so we could be all to gather and we did.

Ole Anderson and Knud Knutson came down in an Oxwagon to help to move ous and we had our Oxwagon so we cared all our house hold on three Wagons we started in May in the year 1873 it taken us two weeks to come out her we arived at Ole Anderson about 6 PM in Bee Creek. and next day we moved to Knut Knutson on Meridian Creek. And we stayed thir till next fall. and we bought A tract of Land of 300 Acers on Eustanr. We built a House on the land and brok land for a farm. And all 5 ov ous lived thir with Mother til all the Girls Married, and then Terry Mared, and then we devided the Land betwen Terry & I Terry got the North half and I got the South half, And I built a House on my land in 1882 and Sister Sally and Tom Hasting lived with me one yeaay and I rented it to Pet Pederson one year, and I stayed on the plase and worked part of land.

Mother made her home with me and Terry as she wished and in June 7 1901 she died and was buried in St Olaf Cemitary.

December 17 -1885 I Married Annie Lee, and we lieved a verry happy Life in the Mustang community about 23 years and We was blest with 6 Childrens Names Alice Mary Bettie Selma Alma William Jr whom Died at one year 22 months.

On the 17 day of Oct 1913 we moved to Cranfills Gap to make our final Home the Girls is all Mared 3 living in Gap and 2 on the Farm 3 or 4 mils out. And I am so glad all mared find substancil men and best of all married men of thir own Church, and thay are bringing thir Children in to the Lord & his Love. So the Lord has Blest Oes through our way of life. I fermley beleve that through his giding hand that I have bin so richley blest, and I Pray that his giding hand may gide me the balance of my Life, Is my Prayer.

This March 1933 W. T. Tergerson.

1921-08-26 KNIGHTS OF THE KU KLUX PARADE AT HILLSBORO

Hillsboro, Texas, August 20 From the dark precincts of a suburban grave yard a long line of white clad Knights of the Ku Klux Klan, following a flaming torch, emerged here tonight and solemnly paraded through the streets, while hundreds of people stood at attention along the sidewalks.

After circling the downtown streets, the procession returned to the graveyard and dispersed. There was(sic) 105 knights in the line with the leader carrying the torch and others carrying banners of warning to evil doers. [my George Bush-inspired italics] As the procession passed a downtown corner, a notice outlining the object of the local Klan was tacked on a post. No one interrupted the march. A few curious stragglers followed the marchers to the graveyard, but did not dare to approach too near.

This is the first demonstration the Knights of Ku Klux Klan have given here.

1921-10-07 SHERIFF AND EIGHT OTHER MEN WOUNDED AT LORENA

Waco, Texas, Oct. 4th. Early this morning, at the sanitarium where Sheriff Buchanan was taken when he arrived here, it was stated that he was to be operated on; that his condition was serious and that he might not survive.

Sheriff Bob Buchanan and Louis Crow of Waco, Carl West of Lorena and Will Lawson were seriously wounded Saturday night at Lorena, thirteen miles south of Waco, while a Ku Klux Klan parade was in progress. Buchanan is wounded under the right arm and Crow is stabbed in the right breast. West is shot in the neck. Five other men were more or less seriously hurt.

Those known to have been wounded in the fight are:

  • Sheriff Bob Buchanan of McLennan County, bullet wounds in the right side and above the right knee.
  • Louis Crow of Waco, proprietor of a local laundry, knife wound in the right side.
  • Carl West, Lorena, bullet wound in the neck.
  • Will Lawson, Lorena, knife wounds.
  • Ed Johnson, Waco, knife wound.
  • Julian Miner, Waco, knife wound.
  • Will Austin, Lorena, knife wound.
  • Charlie Westbrook, Lorena, knife wound.
  • L. S. Dumas, Waco, bullet wound.

Sheriff Buchanan, with Deputies I. Mack Wood and M. Burton, reached Lorena Saturday evening, it having been announced that he parade would begin at 8:30 o'clock. The Sheriff soon after arriving in Lorena, notified those who seemed to have charge of arrangements for the parade that the procession could not take place.

It was thought at first that the parade would be called off and the shooting did not begin until an hour and a half after the hour announced. About fifty white robed figures had passed down the main street, then someone began shooting, followed by a fusillade of shots. When the smoke cleared away nine men, including Sheriff Buchanan, Messrs, Crow and West were found to be wounded.

Ambulances were sent from Waco to Lorena and the wounded were brought to hospitals here.

"Just tell them that the whitecaps shot me," Sheriff Bob Buchanan said. To newspaper men while sitting on the edge of a cot at a local hospital waiting to have his wound dressed.

"I begged and I pleaded with them" he said, "but they would not hear me"

Sheriff Buchanan's wounds appeared to consist of a ball which penetrated under the right armpit and a grazed wound across the right thigh. In answer to a question from Jailer Chapline as to whether he had spit any blood he replied in a firm negative.

Sheriff Buchanan looked confused. "It started --- ", he said vaguely. "I begged and pleaded," he said. It was plain he was too weak from excitement and loss of blood to continue.

Waco parties returning from Lorena late tonight said the trouble began when an officer seized the American flag and the man carrying it, at the head of the parade. The man carrying the flag was unmasked, and some of the others at the head of the parade were not masked. The flag was recovered by men in line and it was carried in the procession. Louis Crow of Waco, one of the men wounded, was not in the parade, being one of the spectators who went from Waco, several thousand from here going to Lorena to witness the Ku Klux parade.

Carl West of Lorena, who was shot in the neck, was brought here tonight for treatment, and another man whose name has not yet been learned. Will Lawson of Lorena who was slightly cut on the neck was not brought to Waco.

The trouble at Lorena Saturday night started after Sheriff Buchanan had repeatedly declared that the parade should not take place in that community. It was thought at first that his edict would prove effective, but this was dispelled when someone in the vast assemblage gathered in the main street of Lorena raised the cry. "The klans are coming."

The first section of the parade reached the business section of the town with practically unbroken ranks, save for the disturbance that occurred when Sheriff Buchanan seized the man who carried the flag, and if the advance guard heard the shooting in the rear, they continued marching. It was not until after the klansmen in the first section had been notified that their column had been broken that they disbanded, some of them immediately removing their robes and masks.

CHRISTIAN O. BRONSTAD

Mr. Christian O. Bronstad was born at Romedal, Hedemarken, Norway, August 20th, 1853. His parents were Ole and Christine Bronstad. He attended the school of his native parish and having received the necessary instruction in the Word of God he was confirmed in this baptismal covenant.

During his teens, Christian heard glowing tales of America, the land of opportunity, of prosperity and freedom and of the bright things awaiting a young man there. Thus this young man’s mind was soon settled oncoming to America. So when Mr. Hendrick Dahl visited back in Norway after having been in America, young Christian fell in line with many others, with Mr. Dahl as escort bid his native land, his friends and all his relatives a fond farewell and turned toward America to seek his fortune. The party on leaving Norway set Bosque County, Texas, as their destination. In the first part of November, 1872, Christian Bronstad, the young emigrant, left Norway. In Bordeaux, France the party was held up three days. In Liverpool another delay, this time of eleven days, held them up. From Liverpool the ship Louisiana of the State Line was boarded. About a month was spent on the Atlantic, and for three days all passengers were shut up on account of the storms. Finally in December 19, 1872 this immigrant at the age of 18, arrived in Waco, Texas.: So reads part of the biography read at the Golden Wedding of Mr. And Mrs. Christian O. Bronstad on March 1, 1927.

Mr. Bronstad was employed about two years in the saw mills and flour mills in Valley Mills. Then he took up his duties at the mill at Norway Mills on Neill’s Creek. Meantime his brother, Gulbrand, had joined him; his parents and others of the family coming to America still later.

On march 1, 1877, Mr. Bronstad was united in marriage to Martha Colwick. It was a double wedding the other couple being Dr. O. M. Olson and Margaret Colwick. The Rev. Ole Estren performed the ceremony.

For about two and a half years after this double wedding the ;young miller and his bride lived at Norway Mills. Leaving the ;mill they lived for a short while on the Colwick farm. In January, 1881, they moved to a newly acquired farm in the Harmony community. Mr. Bronstad was a faithful ;member of Our savior’s Lutheran Church of Norse for nearly fifty years. He was a community worker; worked for better schools, better roads, and has been active in promoting and supporting Clifton College.

Then years ago Mr. And Mrs. Bronstad moved to Clifton where they have resided since. On March 1, 1927 occurred the Golden Wedding of Mr. And Mrs. Christian O. Bronstad and Dr. and Mrs O. M. Olson and the Silver Wedding of Mr. And Mrs. C. A. Brown. 1943-10-08

Cpl. G. O. Bronstad Writes on Arab Customs in North Africa

Relatives in Clifton have received the following letter from Cpl G. O. Bronstad, stationed somewhere in North Africa, and parts of it are reprinted below:

"Some time ago on my afternoon off while roaming around I ran into some Arabs threshing wheat which was very interesting to me as I had heard about it many times but never had the opportunity to see it. Wheat is one of the main crops here although they do not raise a great deal but what I have seen has been fairly good grade. From the best information I can obtain it seems as they made between 30 to 40 bushels to the acre which is about an average year. In many of the larger places here they have reapers of the same make as we have in the States. The threshing I was was done by hand. The Arabs would first cut the wheat by hand and then pile it into large stacks to dry for a few days. There is not much danger of rain since it hardly ever rains here at this time of the year. After it has dried thoroughly they take three horses and walk them around in the pile for about five or ten minutes. Then several Arabs walk into the pile and gather up the straw and let the wind blow through it a few times and then piled all the straw into another stack. They go through this procedure three times and Arabs with sacks or a reasonable facsimile gather this wheat from the ground what what I mean they really get every grain. The straw is then carried home for feeding purposes. You probably think the wheat is dirty after picking it from the ground, well it is, but they are not through with it. The way I have seen them clean it is on the same order as we sift sand but they do get it comparatively clean considering the way they have to clean it. Now the larger farms have reapers as I have mentioned before and how they use them on some of these wheat fields along the hill side is beyond me. Labor is very cheap over here, that is Arabs, as they can be hired for only a few francs a day. A franc is equivalent to two cents.

I also ran across some Arabs shelling corn. This consists of several Arabs around a large pile of corn shucking it by hand. It is then carried over to a place where two Arabs are taking their turns with the crank on the hand sheller which is American made. Now I am sure they have some modern corn shellers here some place as there is plenty of American machinery here including tractors and such.

Last week on my day off, I again journeyed out and attempted to visit the flour mill but didn't have any luck as usual. I did get to see their Bran which is a very poor grade of feed. Their best grade of flour is very coarse but fairly white considering everything. I did look in on the Roll floor and above each roll stand they have a large glass cylinder which the flour runs through before it hits the rolls. Don't know just how many stands they have but it looked like quite a few. I still have hopes of going through it one of these days. On my way back I came across what looked like a large market square which consisted of mostly wheat along with some Corn, Oats, Barley and different kinds of beans. Most of the grain was very clean as it had either been hand picked or cleaned with a hand machine. I saw several Arabs (mostly women) cleaning the wheat by hand moving each grain and picking out the rocks and trash. Very slow cleaning but I suppose they have plenty of time. Some of the wheat, I believe I would be safe in saying, was at least half inch long and seemed to be very heavy. This wheat is very hard and looks very much like what we call Spring Wheat. The corn is a yellow corn but it is small and practically round

We have had a couple of swell feeds lately. Last night we had fried chicken, potato salad, cream corn, vegetable salad, pineapple cream pie and beer or iced tea. They are really truing their best to keep us from going hungry and doing a very good job of it."

What a careful, capable observer! Regretably I never really knew my oldest cousin, Leroy was 24 years older than me, old enough to be my Father, his life removed from mine in its own trajectory.

1953-03-20    W. T. Tergerson Dies At Gap Mar 12

A pioneer citizen of Bosque county and a man well-known throughout Central Texas, William Telef Tergerson, 94 died at his home in Cranfills Gap on Thursday, March 12. Mr. Tergerson had not been in good health for a number of years, but he had been seriously ill for only a week prior to his demise

A son of the late Telef and Asber Tergerson, Mr. Tergerson was born on February 1, 1859, at Brownsboro in Henderson County. It was there that he was baptized on February 27, 1895[sic], into the Lutheran church, and it was in Our Saviour's Lutheran Church at Norse on June 2, 1876, that he was confirmed in his baptismal covenant by the late Reverend Herman Estrem.

On December 17, 1885, Mr. Tergerson married Miss Annie Lee, and to their union six children were born--five daughters, Alice, Mary, Bettie, Selma, and Alma, and one son, William Alexander, who died in infancy. Mrs. Tergerson preceded her husband in death on October 26, 1951.

Mr. Tergerson spent his childhood in Brownsboro and came to Bosque county in 1873. He was engaged in farming in the Mustang community until 1913 at which time he, his wife, and two daughters moved into Cranfills Gap where he resided for the remainder of his life.

Instrumental in organizing the town of Cranfills gap and several business enterprises in that community, Mr. Tergerson for many years was president of the First Security State Bank there.

During the years he always took an active part in the activities concerned with the St. Olaf Lutheran church in Cranfills gap and held important positions of trust and confidence in the Church.

A man who lived a long life with honor and integrity, Mr. Tergerson was beloved by everyone in his community and also counted as his many friends numerous persons throughout Texas.

Left to survive Mr. Tergerson are his five daughters, Mesdames Alice Bertelsen, of fort Worth, and Mary Hansen, Bettie Larson, Selma Bronstad and Alma Rohne, of Cranfills Gap; 18 grandchildren; 22 great-grandchildren; and one great, great-grandchild. . . .
