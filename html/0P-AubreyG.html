Will and Ethel's first child was a son, William Embrey Walton. One presumes Ethel Walton was so delighted with this beautiful daughter, she wanted a portrait.

A beautiful woman, reminiscent of Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis

Fate heaved a heavy burden on Aubrey Irene Walton; tuberculosis threatened her life.
 
Before antibiotics, and better understaning of the disease, treatment, diagnosis and prognosis were poor. She spent time in a sanitorium; Will Walton built a box framed room, part of his house, attached at the west end of the front porch. This arrangement kept her close to help her heal, but distant to protect Will and Ethel from contagion.
 
Aubrey painted the interior of her two window, sliding door box white. With her hand dipped in green paint she decorated the interior with green hand prints, saying, "I yet live".
 
She became well enough to resume teaching. Health considerations made Fresno California a fit choice. Like the semi-quarantine existence in her box, her distant location in California made her the most isolated, remote of her siblings.
