Marshall was killed in an auto accident.



This radicly altered the life of Bob's Mother, Louise. She had to become bread winner, on top of her other roles. Midst the great depression, competition for jobs was severe. She had to gain more teaching credentials to improve hers which were two year Normal school status.

Bob's grandparents, aunts, uncle and Louise's cousins, living hundreds of miles to the West, would have wanted to know what their newest family member looked like.

This is a capsule of their lives. Louise needed autonomy and freedom of an auto to further her education, to access work at remote locations while keeping self and son safe.

Louise and Bob found time for a little needed whimsy.

Where did that collar come from?

Durable coveralls were every day wear for young boys.

Coveralls are still in vogue. Many of Bob's fellow classmates would have been farm kids; they likely dressed the same.

His extra-wide face suggests photographic manipulation. The background looks a bit like the background of figure 7, hasty trowel marks.

At about this age or a little later, Bob put his hard earned harvesting cash into his first car: Bernadine. He couldn't afford a convertible, so he converted the Model A he bought by cutting off it's top.

Bernadine was no beauty, but with all the love Bob put into her, you couldn't help but love her.

A rose Homer and Travis planted had an exceptional year. Gives meaning to a trip Mother and I took to the countryside to gather oak leaf mulch.

Bob went to Clifton Lutheran Junior College for his firset two years then to Texas Weslyan College in Fort Worth for the next two.

Following Mother's example, Bob studied to become a teacher. His teaching practicum taught him otherwise.

Bob began working at Convair Aircraft; he flourished there, moving from fabrication to design.

Bob partnered with one co-worker at Convair, Bob V Langley, in building a drag racing car.

Announcers at the drag strip called her "Old Needle-nose" too.

Bob Langley's share of the partnership was the body and chassis; he was a whiz at welding aluminum. Bob Holbrooks share was the engine.

In drag racing, engines blew up more than bodies smashed; Bob Holbrook grew disillusioned with the partnership, and he was ready to move on.

Bob bowed out of the drag racing partnership with Bob Langley. Louise and Clyde added on a room and bath at back plus a two car garage workshop where Bob salvaged his hot rod engine into a '42 Ford coupe.

Over a span of months, Bob lovingly lavished The '42 Ford with hours of bodywork, pounds of lead, many coats of pink pearl paint and a pricey rolled naugahyde interior to make it a fitting vessel for his (formerly dragster) Cadillac engine blessed with much pricey speed shop equiipment.

For a while, in addition to working at Convair Aircraft, Bob tried a side line of buying and reselling cars. This did not work out for him. Eventually he quit Convair, and went "job shopping". One locale was Salt Lake City.

Bob let me (Walt) use the '55 Studebaker to commute to work and College, '59, '60. He gave it to me as a wedding present Feb 24, 1961.

The Mapp family included Louise's sister, Ina Jo (Walton) Mapp. The two families frequently exchanged visits. This time they are dressed to dine out.

I (Walt) had gotten out of the Army a few months prior, June '69; this is a photo from our visit with Bob and Charlotte.

Mother (Louise) furthered Bob's interest in music, he played the b-flat coronet in high school, toyed with the piano at home and sang in the Clifton Lutheran College Choir. He and his son Mark must have shared interest in the keyboard.

Walt and Pat came many times from Whitmore Lake, MI to visit Bob and Charlotte in Appleton, WI.
