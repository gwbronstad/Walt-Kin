Mrs. Louise W Bronstad Dies

Mrs. Louise Walton Holbrook Bronstad, a retired teacher, died Friday at a Fort Worth hospital. She was 81.

Funeral will be at 1 p.m. tomorrow at Asbury United Methodist Church where she was a member. Burial will be in Rock Church Cemetery, Cranfills Gap, Bosque County. Lucas Funeral Home is in charge of arrangements.

Mrs. Bronstad was born in Hamilton County and had lived in Fort Worth more than 37 years. She was a teacher with the Birdville School District before her retirement. She was a graduate of Tarleton Agricultural College in Stephenville and North Texas State Teachers College in Denton.

She was a member of the Retired Teachers Association and the National Education Association and a former member of the Fort Worth Opera Chorus. She is the widow of Marshall Holbrook.

Survivors: Husband, Clyde P. Bronstad of Clifton, two sons, Bob Holbrook of Appleton, Wis. and Walton Bronstad of Portland, Ore.; one daughter, Bette Bronstad of Fort Worth, one brother, W.E. Walton of Fort Worth two sisters, Annie Joy Walton of Wichita Falls and Ina Jo Mapp of Odessa; seven grand children and two great grandchildren.

The fifth and sixth grades are very glad that Mrs. Bronstad brought her silkworms to school..

Silk worms were one of Mom's successes. Their life–cycle married the magic of nature with the community of man.

Later, post-war years later, after grandmother died, when Aunt Margaret was left to her own devices in the G. O. - Laura Bronstad home, when Clyde's family moved to and lived in that home, there was, somehow, a mulberry tree just a few yards from the front–porch door.

Other people had them. Si Johnson, father of Mark, had a magnificent, fruitful mulberry in the back yard. It seems they had chickens, a small barn and a cow or cows that grazed the pasture through which a little branch or creek wended.

Up the hill a way from the Johnson's, Past Chris Rohne's house, the Bronstad mulberry was too young to yield much fruit, but the leaves could be fed to the silkworms at school and those domesticated silkworms lived their lives as well for us as for any Asian weaver of silk.

They left behind cocoons of white and yellow and, after metamorphosis and mating, tiny bead rows of eggs that yearned for next year's mulberry leaves.

The mulberry could also be pruned for switches that, once your pants were down, could rip painful red whelps on your leg if ten–year–old–you tried to stretch Mother further than she could go.

1943-09-03

Mrs. J. R. Shipp and children recently of San Antonio, and Mrs.Clyde Bronstad and sons of Bell Mead were here Tuesday visiting relatives and friends.

Deracination looks hard on these folks; gasoline rationing should have meant little travel, yet they did travel, keeping family sinews taunt. Workers in the military industry were entitled to 8 gallons of gasoline per week. The round–trip from Bellmead to Cranfills Gap must have cost at least 8 gallons. Clyde still had to get back and forth to Blackland Army Airfield, Louise to Bellmead's LaVega school.

I have a recollection so close to the substance of fiction that I do not recommend the recall as factual; it is surely total fancy. But one wonders, "How do peole behave under conditions of 'excessive economic control?'"

Cpt. Shipp must have received his Captaincy (military commision to pay grade O-3,) because he was an experienced doctor of medicine. {I'm partial to him because he delivered me.} With that combined status he was over-qualified for all the gasoline and tires he needed. An X–card.

Do I recall a mission by Clyde, despite Mother's near withering caution, a mission for Clyde to siphon 10 gallons from Dr. Shipp's car's tank to the '36 Ford's tank?

Bronstads, Shipps and Tindalls played cards together in Cranfills Gap. If Clyde took all the risks – by siphoning the gas from Dr. Shipp's car and compensated his friend for the gas he must buy, how could the good doctor deny that?

Could Clyde do that just to visit his mother? I could not respect him if he could not. But then, this is just frail memory and speculation. His resources may have been adequate without ration–scoffing.

1947-07-18

In a deal that was consummated Tuesday of this week, Mr. E. A. Thompson of Hico sold his farm adjoining town to Mr. R. O. Blum, formerly being the Mrs. M. J. Mickelson place.

Clyde and family lived in the Blum house 1945–1947; it was wonderfully spacious, having more rooms than we could purpose. One or more upstairs rooms was locked, securing others' property, there was a sewing room in which very little was sewn.

In that house, I was first aware of being ill, spending nights on an Army surplus folding cot in the kitchen. Perhaps for warmth, perhaps for medicated steam. In that house, Bob suffered mumps in his adolescence: Dick and I were sternly warned to keep our distance, both to avoid disease and to avoid the wrath of an inexplicably testy Bob. In that house, new–born Bette came home as an infant, separating me from a Mother's attention.

In that house, for the better part of the summer, I owned Mother, all to my own, for Clyde and Bob were away in Kansas and states further North, earning money on wheat harvesting crew.

In that house, Mother taught me love of the pussy–willow catkins bulging day by day in spring warmth, until the slough of spring hail surrounded the house and shocked the pussy–willow into pause.

In that house, I now think, I witnessed a Mother making her nest for her new chick.

More reading between the lines. More putting two plus two together and risking the result be 222. But the sequence of events - Clyde losing his partner, Uncle Homer, Clyde's speculating on housing in Cranfills Gap, of all inauspicious places for spec housing, and the prospects of a partnership with Barton Peterson, in union with visits with his brother–in–law, Sam Hudson Walton, who had grown wealthy with his spudder during wartime need for oil, might be willing to make a familial loan to his brother–in–law suggests. It was not a good time for travelling in North Texas with one's family. In my childhood memory of that winter–return from the visit to Witchita Falls, Texas, freezing rain chased us home much of the way from North to Central Texas. As to the loan, whether fictitious, fruitful or frustrated, the Clyde Bronstad–Barton Peterson partnership worked for a brief while. Worked until the economic absurdity of four grocers vieing for a living in rural, declining Cranfills Gap dictated that any with better outside purchase ought to leave. And unhappily leave, Clyde, years later would.

1948-01-23

Mr. Clyde Bronstad and Mr. Barton Pederson formed a partnership grocery business Monday in purchasing the Clarence Johnson grocery.

The store was in the same building that Clydes Father, Gulbrand, had built. Field limestone walls topped with a flat, roof inclined toward the rear entrance. The front two-thirds began with two display window and a front double screen door. The windows might display perishable fruit like banannas or apples. Sometimes trace inducement schemes were displayed. A given amount of purchases might entitle one to a china plate or a gravy boat. The floors were pine, darkened from decades of sweeping the floors with a mixture of sawdust and kerosene after closing. There were 2 soda pop coolers, chilled with ice and an ice cream cabinet chilled with mechanical refrigeration. A cylindrical copper fire extinguisher hung on the West wall. Front center had an island with cabinets holding cigarettes and other tobacco products. Atop the island, a large cash register commanded commerce. Wooden display cabinets held containters of staples like baking powder or steel canned vegetable or fruit products. Tansverse in the rear of the sales area was a chilled cabinet for displaying cuts of meat. Behind that a butcher's block and a slicing machine. This was mostly Clyde's domain; one of the skills he picked-up was butchering.
